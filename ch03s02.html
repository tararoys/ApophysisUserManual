Barnsley's Fern

So let me give you another, slightly more complex fractal from deep in fractal history. This fractal is called a Barnsley's fern, because it was created by Barnsley and it looks like a fern. Funny how that works, eh?

Here's the base triangle, and the second iteration. Note how the first triangle is only slightly smaller than the base triangle, is move up, and is rotated slightly left. The second and third triangles are mirrors of each other, and are a strange shape. The fourth triangle is so thin it might as well be a line.

Now, let's look at the third iteration, and compare it to the second iteration. Let's take a look at the second iterations' red triangle. Since the second iteration is slightly rotated, the red triangles of the third iteration will be rotated the same amount. It will also be slightly smaller than the triangle it is using as a base. Note that in the third iteration there are four red triangles, one for each of the triangles in the previous iteration.

Now let's look at in the green triangle, i.e. the right-hand leaf. It is not only rotated, but is stretched to be fairly skinny. That means all of the triangles in the third iteration using this triangle as a reference will also be stretched into a skinny triangle. There are also four of them, in the third iteration, one for each of the triangles in the previous iteration

The purple triangle is the same as the second triangle, except for one important feature- it is mirrored. It is this purple triangle that gives the fern its symmetry. Note in the third iteration how the branch that grew from the left-hand leaf (the green triangle) in the second iteration is mirrored when compared to the rest of the branches.

Finally, take a look at the blue triangle. It is essentially a skinny line. It shrinks into progressively smaller skinny lines.

So where does this all lead? To Barnsley's fern. As you can see, iterated function systems can get pretty complicated, pretty quick. However, the transform editor is based off of IFS, so knowing how they work is a huge key to unlocking the secrets of Apophysis.

Now, note that these two examples were linear fractals. This is because it is easiest to see how iterated function systems work with linear fractals. I completely ignored the other variations. Well- to be fair, I didn't completely ignore them. I am saving them for another tutorial, because they do strange and funky things to the fractal, and it is difficult to explain variations and MRCM at the same time.)

So, enjoy Apophysis until next time!
