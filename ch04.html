Setting up the Quilt

A tutorial for Apophysis 2.05 beta 2

I love colors, and I love quilts. Fortunately, Apophysis offers me an opportunity to play with both. Coloring Apophysis fractals is an interesting challenge. In this tutorial I'll explain how Apophysis coloring schemes work and how to get the most out of your fractal.

First, we are going to make this tutorial a bit easier to follow. Click the options button to open the Options menu. Click on the Editor tab. You should see an options window that looks like the picture above. Under Editor Graph, make sure "use transform color" is NOT checked. Under Reference Triangle, make sure Normal is checked. Under Editor Defaults, check both Extended Edit Mode and Lock Transform Axis. Leave the rest of the settings as they are. (Of course, you can change the color of the background and of the grid colors, but for this tutorial I will be using the presets. )

So what did you just do? Take a look at the picture below. See the triangles? There's four of them- red, yellow, blue, and green. When you unchecked 'Use transform colors," you made sure that when you open the Transform Editor, the triangles will be these bright, very visible colors. If "use transform colors" is checked, the triangles often turn some very hard-to-see colors. Also, something important happended when you checked "normal" under reference triangle. The reference triangle is a large grey triangle that would be visible in the picture below if it weren't being covered up by the yellow, green, and blue triangles. When "normal" is checked, this triangle will not randomly change its shape. If you check the other options, this triangle can change shape and often wanders all over the screen in a very annoying manner.

I'm going to color a fractal I call Serpinski's quilt. It's exactly like the Serpinski's triangle mentioned in tutorial 2, with one additional triangle.

Here's how to make a Serpinski's Quilt of your own. Open the transform editor,

and arrange your fractals to look like the transform editor pictured above. Remember, the reference triangle- that big, white, non-moving triangle- is covered by the yellow, blue, and green triangles. Now, listen carefully. The red, yellow, blue and green triangles are called transforms, and that's what I will be calling them for the rest of the tutorial. So don't get confused about the transform stuff- I'm just talking about the triangles that you can move in the transform editor. That's why it's called a transform editor, yes?

Of course, if you don't want to move transforms around, you can type the exact values (pictured below) into the transform tab of the transform editor. Remember, these coordinates are for the transform tab, NOT the triangle tab. Yes, these coordinates are different. Maybe in another tutorial I'll cover exactly why.
