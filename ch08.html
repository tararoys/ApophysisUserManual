Introduction

Note: When I first wrote this tutorial, parts of it required a version of Apophysis with the square variation. It has come to my attention that most versions of Apophysis don't have the sinusoidal variation. Much the same result can be had by substituting the square variation for the sinusoidal variation and scaling the transform up by about a thousand percent. The pictures are created using the square variation because I haven't found the time to go back and redo them all with the sinusoidal variation. Thus, the ones you make with the sinusoidal variation will probably not look as smooth, but will have some interesting patterns in the squares anyway.

Post transforms are yet another step in our MRCM. We learned about the first step in the MRCM machine in previous chapters, and we learned about the second step in MRCM the chapter before this. Now, in this tutorial, we get to learn about post transforms, the third step in our MRCM. Yay!

So what, exactly, to post transforms do? They let you move, reshape, and rotate elements after you've already added a variation to them. Hence the name 'post-transform'- it means 'to move after.'

Why are these useful? Lots of reasons- but it's easier to see if I actually show it.
