Setting up

Start off with a new blank flame. Hit this button in the transform editor in order to get it.

This will give you a one-transform fractal with the linear variation. Change the linear variation to the sinusoidal variation. Set the sinusoidal variation on both transforms to 1, and make sure to set the linear variation to zero. You will see a fractal that looks like the picture on the top and a transform that looks like the picture on the bottom.

On the main screen, you will see a sinusoidal fractal. This is because the sinusoidal variation creates a sinusoidal right in the middle of the screen. In the transform editor, try moving the transforms around. You may see the fractal moving around, but note that no matter where you move the transform, it always stays within the bounds of a square. This is what the sinusoidal variation does- it just creates a square boundary that doesn't depend on the transform's position, rotation, skew, or anything.

Well- that's just great. As it is, the sinusoidal variation seems to be pretty darned boring. What can we do with it? That's simple. The secret to moving the sinusoidal around is to do it using post transforms.
