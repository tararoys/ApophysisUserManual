Symmetry

All right. Get a new gradient and go back to setting only the top right-hand triangle color slider to one. We are going to play with they symmetry setting. Now, unfortunately, I don't have a clever explanation for the symmetry property, quite simply because I don't really know how it works. However, I can tell you how I think it works, and give you a few simple dos and don'ts. Go to the top right-hand triangle and set its symmetry value to one.

Boooooring! Rule of symmetry #1- don't put symmetry on a transform set to one. Having both color and symmetry on the same transform generally leads to a boring color scheme. Set it back to zero. Now, go to the lower left-hand triangle. Leave its color slider at zero, but type in a symmetry of one.

Ooh, pretty! Now, I'll tell you what I think is happening with symmetry. When you set the lower-left hand's transform value to 1, it is telling the lower left-hand transform to make an exact copy of the entire fractal. Look at the lower left-hand fourth of this quilt. It is, indeed a miniature of the entire fractal. Here's a bit more in depth explanation

Now, look at the transforms. The yellow and the green transform don't have any color or symmetry settings. Because of this, they do what they always do- they copy reference triangle without any color. The red transform has its color slider set all the way to the left, so it copies the reference triangle and adds some color. But, by far the most interesting things happen in the green transform. The green transform, as you recall, has it's symmetry value set to 1.
